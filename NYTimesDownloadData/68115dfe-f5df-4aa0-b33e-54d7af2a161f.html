    From a Photograph in Brussels, to Relief in India

    Covered in debris and blood, with a shocked expression on her face, Nidhi Chaphekar, a 40-year-old flight attendant and mother of two from Mumbai, India, became one of the most vivid symbols of the carnage after her photograph appeared prominently in several major news outlets, including on the front page of The New York Times.

    The woman who took the photo, Ketevan Kardava, told USA Today that she was less than six feet away from the first explosion while on her way to an assignment in Geneva.

    “When I realized that I was alive, the first thing I did was take photo,” Ms. Kardava told the newspaper. “You know, a journalist’s job. The first photo I took was the woman in the yellow jacket.”

    Ms. Kardava, who works for the national public broadcaster in Georgia, said she wrestled with how to balance taking photographs with seeking help for the injured.

    “I was shouting, ‘Doctor! Doctor! Doctor!’ And no one was there,” she said. “What do you do in this situation if you’re a journalist? Help? Ask doctor to come? Or take a photo?”

    She added, “In that very moment, I realized that to show the world what was happening in this moment of terror, a photo was more important.”

    Her family in India saw the photograph while reading news coverage of the blasts, her brother-in-law Nilesh Chaphekar told The Associated Press.

    “The first reaction was: ‘She’s alive. By God’s grace, she is alive,’ ” he said.

    Streets Are Quiet in a Traumatized Brussels

    The center of Brussels was eerily quiet Thursday morning. Usually bustling streets near the historic center were sparsely populated. Soldiers in fatigues rode through the chic Place du Grand Sablon on an army truck, an incongruous sight on a square known for its homemade truffle emporiums and upmarket luxury shops. Flags on buildings were at half-staff.

    “People are demoralized,” Abdullah el-Katrani, a taxi driver, said. “People are not going out unless it’s necessary. They are still in shock.”

    He said there had been few customers since the attacks on Tuesday.

    Mr. Katrani, who comes from Molenbeek, a heavily immigrant neighborhood in Brussels that has become hotbed of radicalism, said people were traumatized.

    “To see our neighborhood portrayed like this, to see what happened, people are shocked,” he said. “These youth are lost. They have nothing to do with most of us. Most of us have tears in our eyes.”

    Hillary Clinton’s Call to Rethink Soft-Target Protection Puzzles Some Experts

    In a speech at Stanford University in California on Wednesday, Hillary Clinton mentioned specific lessons that could be drawn from the Brussels attacks, including a need to reevaluate the way that soft targets could be protected from similar attacks in the future.

    “We need to take a harder look at security protocols at airports and other sensitive so-called soft sites — especially areas outside guarded perimeters,” she said.

    That call was puzzling to some security experts, who said that the specific challenge of protecting soft sites was difficult to isolate from the larger problem of foiling terrorist attacks.

    “If you sort of look at what terrorists do, they have always preferred to go after civilian soft targets,” said Christine Fair, a professor at the Peace and Security Studies Program at Georgetown University. “The whole point is to terrify a public.”

    She added, “The definition of a terrorist organization or a terrorist attack is the deliberate targeting of noncombatants.”

    William Braniff, the executive director of the National Consortium for the Study of Terrorism and Responses to Terrorism, agreed that it would be difficult to prevent such attacks.

    “Soft targets are soft targets for a reason,” he said. “There are just tensions between security and efficiency, particularly in transportation.”

    But he also said that there were opportunities for potential targets to improve their ability to deter terrorists. For instance, he suggested that deploying mobile security teams in transportation hubs and sports arenas could prove more unpredictable for potential attackers than a stationary security checkpoint.

    “If you had a very strict plan in mind and you have someone who has to talk to a security officer, you can force people to alter their plans or make mistakes,” he said.

    Attacks on airports and other transportation hubs have declined in the West in the last decade, according to an analysis of information from the Global Terrorism Database conducted by the website FiveThirtyEight.

    But additional security measures that have made those gathering places safer could be difficult to extend to the broad array of venues that would be attractive to potential attackers.

    Ms. Fair said the best solutions were pre-emptive ones.

    “What you really need is effective law enforcement and effective intelligence,” she said.

    New Information May Tie Brussels Suspects to ISIS and the Paris Attackers

    Though the brothers who have been identified as two of the suicide bombers in the Brussels attacks are said to have had a history of violent crime, Belgian officials said they had no reason to suspect that they were terrorists until recently.

    President Recep Tayyip Erdogan of Turkey contradicted that on Wednesday.

    He said that one of the brothers, Ibrahim el-Bakraoui, 29, had been detained near the Syrian border last June and that the Turkish government had told Belgian authorities he “was a foreign terrorist fighter,” and then deported him.

    “Despite our warnings that this person was a foreign terrorist fighter, the Belgian authorities could not identify a link to terrorism,” Mr. Erdogan said on Wednesday.

    This adds to the expanding profiles of the men suspected of carrying out the terrorist attacks that killed more than 30 people.

    The Outsize Role of Brothers in Terrorist Plots

    In terrorist attacks around the world, pairs of brothers keep turning up as suspects.

    • Ibrahim el-Bakraoui, 29, and another man blew themselves up at Brussels Airport on Tuesday morning.
    • Ibrahim el-Bakraoui’s younger brother Khalid el-Bakraoui, 27, carried out a suicide attack at the Maelbeek subway station.
    • Authorities are trying to determine whether the other suicide bomber at the airport was Najim Laachraoui, 24, another Belgian.

    The Belgian authorities are trying to establish firm ties between the three men and the Paris attackers, including Salah Abdeslam, who was arrested last week; Abdelhamid Abaaoud, the suspected architect of the Paris attacks, who was killed by police in November; and Bilal Hadfi, who died carrying out the Paris terror operation.

    Transportation Problems Persist in Brussels

    Traveling in and out of Brussels remains difficult in the wake of the terrorist attacks.

    Flights to and from the city have not resumed. In a statement Wednesday, the Brussels Airport said that until it could assess the damage to its terminal from the blasts, “it remains unclear” when it would reopen.

    In a statement posted to its website, the international airport said that there would be no flights on Thursday or Friday. Passengers have been advised to contact their airline for alternative travel routes.

    The Brussels subway system was operating on a limited basis Wednesday and officially ceased operations at 7 p.m. local time. Bus lines were also limited.

    The city’s public transport operator, the Brussels Intermunicipal Transport company (STIB), has released more detailed information about operations on its website. It announced that most bus and tram lines would operate on Thursday, and that limits to the subway schedule would be similar to those on Wednesday.
