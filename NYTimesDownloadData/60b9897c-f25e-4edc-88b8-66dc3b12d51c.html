    A Day of Disclosures

    The debate on Monday in the House of Commons over the fallout from the Panama Papers lasted 1 hour, 27 minutes and 21 seconds.

    No smoking guns were revealed in Prime Minister David Cameron’s personal finances, or in the finances of other officials. And there were no major policy announcements.

    Still, the parliamentary debate was the most vigorous discussion of the use of offshore bank accounts and shell companies to conceal personal wealth or avoid taxes since the Panama Papers were released.

    It was also a chance for Britain’s two main parties to lay out their competing visions.

    Mr. Cameron defended his father’s use of offshore funds and — after an initially clumsy response to reports based on the leaked documents from a Panamanian law firm — he said he had now disclosed more about his personal finances than any of his predecessors.

    Before and After the Panama Papers: David Cameron of Britain on Tax Avoidance

    By KIMIKO DE FREYTAS-TAMURA

    The prime minister has condemned offshore accounts used to avoid taxes, but leaked documents show that his family has profited from an offshore investment fund.

    Mr. Cameron also promised new criminal penalties for companies that fail to prevent their employees from facilitating tax evasion, and promised greater transparency by British territories and dependencies that are often used as tax havens.

    The opposition Labour Party, whose leader, Jeremy Corbyn, embodies the party’s leftward shift, failed to score any knockout punches, but underscored a basic theme that has resonated with voters on both sides of the Atlantic: The rules of the game are rigged to favor “the superrich,” as Mr. Corbyn put it. And at least one of his parliamentary allies drew connections between that perceived unfairness and London’s soaring home prices — perhaps the most pressing issue as the capital prepares for mayoral elections next month.

    Mr. Cameron’s Conservatives defended themselves as the party of people who want to get ahead in life; the prime minister called the Labour Party an “enemy of aspiration,” and one lawmaker decried the Labour Party’s “politics of envy.”

    David Cameron Releases Tax Data After Panama Papers Uproar

    Mr. Cameron, the British prime minister, admitted that he had bungled his response to questions about his inheritance from his father, who had an offshore investment company.

    Although the disclosure rules have not changed, the public debate has set off a race to release tax returns. Nicola Sturgeon, the most powerful politician in Scotland, shared her finances over the weekend, and she was quickly joined by Mr. Cameron; Mr. Corbyn; George Osborne, who as chancellor of the Exchequer is the second-most powerful figure in government; and Mayor Boris Johnson of London.

    What’s Going On With Anguilla and Guernsey

    One of the most substantive policy announcements to emerge from Monday’s parliamentary debate involves British dependencies and territories that are often used to create offshore companies for tax purposes.

    Prime Minister David Cameron said on Monday that the majority of these jurisdictions have agreed to compile information on offshore companies registered there; to share the information among themselves; and to create centralized registries of “beneficial ownership” — the individuals and entities that actually benefit from a company that is held under someone else’s name. (Some reformers want Mr. Cameron to go further, by insisting that those registries be made public.)

    The jurisdictions that have agreed to these steps include Bermuda, the British Virgin Islands, the Cayman Islands, Gibraltar, the Isle of Man, Jersey, Montserrat and Turks and Caicos.

    Two, however, have not: Anguilla and Guernsey. Mr. Cameron noted several times on Monday that they were exceptions to the new standards his government supports. He did not explain why they were holding out, but expressed hope that they would fall in line.

    Anguilla, a popular tax haven, is a British overseas territory in the Caribbean; it is part of the United Kingdom but has its own legislature.

    Guernsey, in the English Channel off the coast of Normandy, is one of three Crown dependencies, along with Jersey and the Isle of Man. Officially, it is a self-governing possession of the Crown, and not part of the United Kingdom.

    In practice, the British government has overwhelming influence over both the overseas territories and the Crown dependencies, so it would be difficult for them to resist London’s authority, if the government were to ultimately insist that they adopt uniform policies. How much London will insist on such uniformity — and whether the overhaul will be sufficient — remain matters of debate.

    A Public Debate: Class and Privilege in Britain

    Monday afternoon’s debate in Parliament over the Panama Papers scandal was an open discussion of the role of wealth and privilege in Britain.

    Prime Minister David Cameron is a graduate of Eton College, one of the country’s most prestigious boarding schools, and of Oxford University.

    Mr. Cameron had said, in the days since the revelations from the Panama Papers, “I’ve never hidden the fact that I’m a very lucky person.”

    “I had wealthy parents who gave me a great upbringing, who paid for me to go to an amazing school,” he said. “I’ve never tried to be anything that I’m not.”

    Still, the news that his father had an offshore investment company, and that the prime minister had sold shares in the fund at a profit, has been embarrassing for the Conservative Party.

    One member of the party, Michael Fabricant, who represents the Lichfield constituency in Staffordshire in the West Midlands, came to Mr. Cameron’s defense.

    “He should not be ashamed that the had the good fortune to be born into a well-off family,” Mr. Fabricant said. “It’s not a sin for his parents, quite naturally, to want their savings to be cascaded down through the generations. He has nothing to be ashamed of.”

    Mr. Fabricant said: “No matter how much information he wants to divulge, nothing will satisfy some of those people on the Labour front bench.”

    Mr. Cameron used the opportunity to reiterate his earlier position that existing disclosure rules for members of Parliament were adequate. He noted that he had fended off demands that lawmakers and cabinet members be required to disclose all of their personal financial interests.

    While members of the Labour Party have moved further to the left in recent years — notably by electing Jeremy Corbyn, a far-left backbencher, as the party’s leader last year — the Conservative Party has tried to position itself as being in the center of the political spectrum.

    Just this month, Mr. Cameron noted, the government raised the standard personal allowance — the amount of income individuals do not have to pay tax on — to £11,000, or about $15,700, from £10,600.

    Distinguishing Secrecy From Evasion

    Several major themes have arisen in Monday afternoon’s debate over tax sheltering. One is the question of individual tax evaders versus the havens — including the British Virgin Islands — that are seen as facilitating tax avoidance. Another is the question of transparency and disclosure.

    “Can the prime minister guarantee that the law will make offshore tax dodging illegal?” asked Liz Saville Roberts, a member of Parliament with the Welsh party Plaid Cymru.

    Prime Minister David Cameron noted that tax evasion was already illegal and called it an issue separate from the sharing of information across jurisdictions, and the ability to examine who are the owners of companies.

    But he added, “We shouldn’t use that to say that it’s wrong for people or trade unions or companies or pension schemes to invest in unit trusts listed in other countries.”

    Unit trusts, sometimes also known as open-ended funds, allow investors to buy an ownership stake in a range of investments, which are managed by a fund manager. They are common investment vehicles.

    “The unit trust does not exist to make money for itself,” Mr. Cameron said. The investors are the ones who make or lose money, and are responsible for the taxes on any gains, he said.

    But the fact that so many of these investment vehicles are based overseas has itself become politically sensitive — the vast majority of individual investors do not participate in such funds.

    Scottish Party Presses Cameron to Crack Down on Tax Avoidance

    Angus Robertson, the leader of the Scottish National Party’s bloc in Britain’s Parliament, has challenged the prime minister to detail the government’s efforts to crack down on illegal tax shelters.

    The Scots are a significant bloc in Parliament. Mr. Robertson’s colleague Nicola Sturgeon, the party’s leader and the first minister of Scotland, was the first national leader to release her tax returns; her decision to do so, over the weekend, prompted Prime Minister David Cameron and Jeremy Corbyn, the leader of the main opposition, the Labour Party, to do the same.

    Mr. Cameron reiterated a theme of Monday afternoon’s debate: distinguishing illegal trusts from legitimate “unit trusts” that, he said, are a “totally legitimate way of investing” by pension funds and other institutions, among others.

    Mr. Cameron also told Mr. Robertson that he did not envision adding substantial new sections to the criminal code, beyond his proposal — announced minutes earlier — to punish companies that fail to prevent their employees from illegally avoiding taxes.

    Mr. Cameron acknowledged to Mr. Robertson that “for years there was a reputational, and potentially a real, problem” with Britain’s dependencies and territories — like the British Virgin Islands — that have been used as tax shelters or havens for money laundering or graver offenses.

    With the overhaul, Mr. Cameron said, Britain’s territories will by no means be the worst offenders. He said that several states in the United States were even worse. While he did not name any, advocates of financial transparency have long criticized the design of laws in Delaware, Nevada and Wyoming, states often used to incorporate offshore companies and trusts.

    Cameron Says Labour Party Is ‘Enemy of Aspiration’

    Prime Minister David Cameron of Britain, who appeared to have a strong command of policy details, pointed out that Jeremy Corbyn, the Labour Party leader, had himself delayed releasing his tax disclosures until midafternoon Monday.

    Mr. Cameron said he had gone through all of his financial holdings with the cabinet secretary, Sir Jeremy Heywood, covering his financial interests and his relationships with family and friends.

    He said the Labour Party was, under the guise of promoting transparency, pursuing its real agenda of raising the inheritance tax. “If you want to pass your home to your children, they will tax it,” he said, warming to his theme that the Labour Party would crush individual initiative and opportunity.

    “They are the enemy of aspiration” and of families wanting to advance their station in life, Mr. Cameron said, “and that’s the real lesson of today.”

    A Call for Independent Investigation Into the Panama Papers

    Jeremy Corbyn, the leader of the Labour Party, dismissed Prime Minister David Cameron’s proposal of a task force to examine the Panama Papers scandal. The panel would report to George Osborne, the chancellor of the Exchequer, and Theresa May, the home secretary, who Mr. Corbyn said cannot be impartial.

    “A tax task force reporting to the chancellor and the home secretary, both members of a party funded by donors implicated in the Panama leaks, will neither be independent nor credible,” Mr. Corbyn said.

    He said it was disgraceful that there had been just 11 prosecutions over offshore tax evasion since 2010.

    Corbyn Calls Cameron’s Statements ‘A Master Class in the Art of Distraction’

    Video: By THE ASSOCIATED PRESS

    Jeremy Corbyn, who was elected last year to lead the opposition Labour Party, has dismissed Prime Minister David Cameron’s statements as “a master class in the art of distraction,” saying the prime minister had done too little, too late to make the wealthy disclose their assets.

    “There is now one rule for the superrich and another for the rest,” said Mr. Corbyn, a hard-left member of Parliament who represents a district in North London. “How can it be right that street cleaners, teaching assistants and nurses work and pay their taxes, yet some at the top think the rules simply don’t apply to them?”

    Mr. Corbyn said that Mr. Cameron had yet to answer many questions about his assets and his family’s holdings in offshore trusts. He said that Britain, under Mr. Cameron’s Conservative government, had resisted efforts by the European Union to crack down on tax havens.

    “The U.K. is at the heart of the global tax avoidance industry,” Mr. Corbyn said. “It’s a national scandal, and it’s got to end.”

    He said the government was “incapable” of taking serious transnational action against tax avoidance, noting that American companies like Google were pouring money into lobbying efforts in Brussels to protect their large pools of overseas capital from tax collectors.

    New Steps to Combat Tax Evasion

    Britain has been criticized for the role some of its territories — the British Virgin Islands, the Cayman Islands, Bermuda, the Isle of Man and the Isle of Jersey — have played as tax shelters.

    Prime Minister David Cameron said on Monday that these territories had “already agreed to exchange taxpayer financial account information automatically,” starting in September, and that Britain was already going beyond what its counterparts in the Group of 20 advanced economies were doing to combat corruption and opacity.

    Mr. Cameron announced — perhaps most significantly — a “new criminal offense” that will punish corporations “who fail to prevent their representatives from facilitating tax evasion.”

    He also said that a government task force, comprising representatives from Revenue and Customs, the Financial Conduct Authority and other bodies, would be set up to examine the releases from the Panama Papers scandal and determine further steps.

    Cameron Reiterates Apology for Being Slow on Disclosure

    Prime Minister David Cameron of Britain said on Monday that many investment trusts are perfectly legitimate, and urged his colleagues to distinguish between trusts that “artificially reduce tax” and “those that encourage investment.”

    Those that encourage investment, he said, are perfectly legal.

    He said he had sold his share of his family’s trust in 2010 — the year his party came to power — “because I didn’t want any issues about conflicts of interest,” and added that he “didn’t want anybody to be able to suggest that as prime minister” he would have any outside interests.

    “I accept all of the criticisms,” he said, for being slow to respond to the Panama Papers disclosures about his father’s offshore trust. He added that the delay was a result of his strong emotions about what he deemed unfair coverage of his father, Ian, who died in 2010.

    “He was a hard-working man and a wonderful dad, and I’m proud of everything he did to build a business and provide for his family,” the prime minister said.

    Cameron Defends Existing Disclosure Rules

    Prime Minister David Cameron began the debate on Monday by arguing that existing disclosures rules were sufficient, and stating that he and his top deputy, George Osborne, the chancellor of the Exchequer, had been completely transparent.

    “Dealing with my own circumstances first, yesterday I published all of the information in my tax returns, not just for the last year but for the last six years,” he said. He added that he had produced additional information about his salary and inheritances, including his salary; the in-kind benefit from living at 10 Downing Street, the prime minister’s residence; his and his wife’s payments as leaders of the Conservative Party; and rental and interest income.

    “The publication of the prime minister’s tax information in this way is unprecedented, but I think it is the right thing to do,” he said. “But let me be clear. I’m not suggesting that this should apply to all MPs.”

    It would be a mistake, he said, to completely abandon taxpayer privacy rights for members of Parliament, as some advocates have suggested.

    His address was greeted with a mix of cheers and jeers, as is common in parliamentary debates, particularly during Prime Minister’s Questions sessions.

    Cameron Plans to Propose New Penalties on Tax Evasion

    Prime Minister David Cameron of Britain, who has been under pressure after disclosures about an offshore company set up by his father, plans to propose on Monday new criminal penalties for companies that fail to stop their employees from using offshore companies to avoid taxes.

    The proposal — opposed by some banks, accounting firms and law firms, which say that the law would hurt London’s role as a global financial center — is expected to be Mr. Cameron’s most robust response so far to the Panama Papers scandal, which has exposed vast and secretive holdings of wealth by powerful people around the world.

    Jeremy Corbyn, the leader of the opposition Labour Party, is expected to continue pressing Mr. Cameron, arguing that the prime minister has failed to adequately explain his family’s involvement with offshore tax increases. Nicola Sturgeon, the first minister of Scotland and the leader of the Scottish National Party, published her tax return over the weekend.

    On Monday morning, the government announced that George Osborne, the chancellor of the Exchequer, the second most powerful government job after prime minister, would publish his tax returns.
